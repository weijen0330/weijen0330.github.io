Molly's Caribbean

Updated the website for a small company based in Seattle that sells honeymoon packages to newlyweds. My team and I cleaned up their HTML and CSS code, made the site mobile friendly, and created two brand new features.

The Challenge

Towards the end of my INFO 343 class (Client-side Web Development), we were asked to turn in a final project. One of my professors ex-students reached out to us and told us that his mom was looking for someone to update the website's look and make it mobile friendly. We were excited about project, but our customer asked us not to mess with the JavaScript, which we wanted to challenge ourselves for the final project, so we decided to implement two new features.

Figuring It Out

After sitting down face-to-face with our client Chris (representing his mom, Molly), together we were able to identify a list of issues that the old website has.

  1. Molly's photo up at the top are all crammed together.
  2. The home page (Picture 1) has two background pictures pieced together in the banner area.
  3. There are an incredible amount of white space on all the pages.
  4. The footer is awkwardly large on all the pages.
  5. The contact/email form (Picture 5) is very outdated. It feels like a phishing site.
  6. Molly meets most of her clients at wedding shows, which are hidden to the side in the "Upcoming Seattle Events" and "Upcoming Portland Events" sections.

As for the mobile side, the words just scale very chaotically. Some of them are too big and some of them are small, making it very hard on the eyes when checking out the website on a smartphone.

The Solution

Here is the new website! The first task on our to-do list was to clean up the code. I noticed that the person who developed the original website would specified the CSS style in the HTML file with a style tag, and even if an element is repeatedly being used, such as the boxes in the FAQ section, he/she would type out the same style for all of them, producing lots of redundancy. To resolve this issue, I simply created a CSS file to house all the style rules. Then, my team and I were able address each of issues we learned from our client one by one. Here how we solved them respectively:

  1. We spaced out the logos out so they are not all jammed in the middle of the page and easier to look at.
  2. We took just one of the photos and blew it up to fit the entire page.
  3. We used the background picture to cover the entire page and place all the content in a dark overlay.
  4. We took away the footer to make the entire page more cohesive.
  5. Our client told us that most people actually don't even used the "Request A Quote" function in the banner area. They usually just emailed her for business inquiries. We took this into consideration and eliminated the email page entirely and switched to a jQuery modal plugin instead. It pops up when customers press "Email Molly", and it allows the customers to quickly return to the main site when they are finished by clicking the "x" button instead of the back button. This new flow makes sense to us since "Email Molly" is so frequently used.
  6. Since our client meets the majority of her customers at wedding shows, we decided to move those dates to the top of the page where they can be prominently displayed rather than hidden to the side, as well as her mission statement.

For mobile, we simply added more CSS rules so that everything scales down nicely. We also wanted to focus on simplicity, which was why we took away most of the pictures on the homepage and left only important information: her logos, "Emailing Molly", mission statement, and wedding show dates.

Our deliverable for Chris and Molly pretty much stopped here. They used WordPress to host this site so if we did anything with JavaScript it would likely be incompatible. However, we wanted to get creative, so we added two new features to the website. Just for fun.

I was in charge of implementing the first feature, which is a face-tracking video booth I called "Picture Yourself In Paradise." I wanted the customers to experience a little piece of the Caribbean from the comfort of sitting in front of their computers. I worked with PhotoShop to manipulate the bikini photo slightly, HTML's camera and video element, JavaScript, as well as an open-source face-tracking library called headtrackr that I found online. (and yes! That's my face in the photo! Not my body though...)

The second one I only assisted, but it is a weather map of the exotic countries that Molly sends her customers to in the Caribbean. We got the idea because Chris told us that the weather in the Caribbean varies greatly, and the customers definitely would not want to be rained on during their honeymoon.

Our clients Molly and Chris really like the final product, as well as the extra features that we created for fun.
